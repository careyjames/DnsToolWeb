Security Policy

Vulnerability Disclosure Policy for DNS Tool

IT Help San Diego Inc. — Effective February 14, 2026

Report a Vulnerability

If you believe you have found a security vulnerability in DNS Tool or any IT Help San Diego service, please report it responsibly:

Email
security@it-help.tech
security.txt
/.well-known/security.txt (RFC 9116)

Preferred language: English. We aim to acknowledge reports within 3 business days.

Scope

This policy applies to the following assets owned and operated by IT Help San Diego Inc.:

Asset Type Status
dnstool.it-help.tech Web Application In Scope
www.it-help.tech Corporate Website In Scope
it-help.tech Domain & DNS Infrastructure In Scope
*.it-help.tech All Subdomains In Scope
Out of Scope
  • Third-party services we integrate with (DNS resolvers, crt.sh, ip-api.com, SecurityTrails)
  • Social engineering, phishing, or physical attacks against IT Help San Diego employees
  • Denial of service (DoS/DDoS) attacks
  • Automated vulnerability scanning that degrades service for other users
  • Findings from domains analyzed by the tool (those belong to their respective owners)
Authorization & Safe Harbor
IT Help San Diego Inc. authorizes good-faith security testing of all in-scope assets listed above, provided that testing complies with this policy. You do not need prior permission to begin testing.

We consider security research conducted under this policy to be:

  • Authorized — in accordance with the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA), and we will not initiate or support legal action against you for accidental, good-faith violations of this policy
  • Exempt from the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) restrictions, and we will not bring claims against you for circumvention of security controls
  • Lawful, helpful, and conducted in the public interest — performed in good faith with the goal of improving security for all users

We ask that you make a good-faith effort to avoid privacy violations, data destruction, and service interruption during your research. If you accidentally access user data, stop immediately, do not store or share it, and report what happened.

What We Ask of Researchers
Do
  • Report vulnerabilities promptly via security@it-help.tech
  • Provide enough detail to reproduce the issue (steps, screenshots, proof-of-concept)
  • Give us reasonable time to address the issue before public disclosure
  • Act in good faith to avoid harm to users, data, and availability
  • Test only against your own accounts or test data
Do Not
  • Access, modify, or delete data belonging to other users
  • Perform attacks that degrade service availability (DoS, resource exhaustion)
  • Use automated scanning tools at volumes that impact other users
  • Publicly disclose details before we have had time to remediate
  • Engage in social engineering, phishing, or physical attacks
Our Commitment
3 days Acknowledgment of your report
14 days Initial assessment and triage
90 days Target remediation window
  • We will work with you to understand and validate the issue
  • We will provide status updates at least every 14 days until the issue is resolved
  • We will keep you informed about our progress toward resolution
  • We will credit researchers who report valid vulnerabilities (unless you prefer to remain anonymous)
  • We will not take legal action against researchers who follow this policy
Coordinated Disclosure

We follow a coordinated disclosure process:

  • Private reporting — vulnerabilities should be reported privately via security@it-help.tech
  • Remediation period — we request a minimum of 90 days from the initial report before any public disclosure, to allow sufficient time for investigation and remediation
  • Mutual agreement — the timing and content of any public disclosure will be coordinated between IT Help San Diego and the researcher
  • Critical vulnerabilities — for issues that pose an imminent threat to users, we will prioritize an expedited fix and may request early coordination on disclosure timing
  • Publication — once resolved, we welcome researchers to publish their findings and will collaborate on accurate technical details

If we are unable to resolve an issue within the 90-day window, we will communicate a revised timeline and work with the researcher to agree on a responsible disclosure date.

Qualifying Vulnerabilities

Examples of issues we consider in scope (not exhaustive):

  • Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)
  • Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF)
  • Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF)
  • SQL Injection or other injection flaws
  • Authentication or authorization bypasses
  • Sensitive data exposure
  • Remote Code Execution (RCE)
  • Insecure Direct Object References (IDOR)
  • Significant security misconfigurations
  • Information disclosure with demonstrable impact
Security Practices

DNS Tool is built with security as a core principle:

  • No user accounts or passwords — no authentication data to compromise
  • No cookies or tracking — minimal data collection reduces attack surface
  • Content Security Policy (CSP) with per-request nonces on all pages
  • CSRF protection on all state-changing operations
  • Rate limiting to prevent abuse
  • SSRF hardening — internal network ranges blocked on outbound requests
  • Input validation — domain names validated and sanitized before processing
  • HTTPS enforced with HSTS and secure headers
  • Open source — core application code is publicly available for review on GitHub
