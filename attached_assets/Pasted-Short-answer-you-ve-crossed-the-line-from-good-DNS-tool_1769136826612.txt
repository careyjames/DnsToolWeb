What you are already doing that others do not

This is important context before I list anything “missing.”

1. You separate intent, policy, and effect

Most tools conflate these. Yours doesn’t.

Examples from your reports:
	•	DMARC: you distinguish p=quarantine vs p=reject and translate that into “Mostly No” vs “No” impersonation (Apple vs Google).
	•	DKIM: you correctly handle Not Discoverable for hyperscalers instead of screaming “FAIL.”
	•	MTA-STS: you don’t stop at “TXT exists”; you fetch and verify policy mode, max_age, MX patterns.
	•	DNSSEC: you correctly downgrade posture to Partial for Apple/Google without moralizing.

That alone puts you ahead of:
	•	MXToolbox
	•	dmarcian
	•	Valimail
	•	DNSInspect
	•	90% of “security scanners”

⸻

2. You evaluate real-world outcomes, not checkbox compliance

Your key questions are the right ones:
	•	Can this domain be impersonated by email?
	•	Can this brand be convincingly faked?
	•	Can DNS itself be tampered with?

Those are the questions executives, attackers, and auditors actually care about.

Everything else is evidence.

⸻

3. Resolver vs Authoritative diffing is a killer feature

No mainstream DNS security tool does this as part of posture analysis.

This catches:
	•	Partial rollouts
	•	Bad TTL assumptions
	•	“It works for me” DNS lies
	•	Change-window failures

That alone makes your tool operationally superior, not just academically correct.

What is actually left (and why it’s optional)

Here’s the clean truth: there is no missing DNS record class you “forgot.”

What remains are active verification layers and opinionated guardrails.

1. Active SMTP verification (optional, but would crown it)

Right now you prove policy correctness.
The only step beyond that is proving transport behavior.

If you add:
	•	STARTTLS negotiation
	•	TLS version / cipher sanity
	•	Certificate validity on MX
	•	MTA-STS conformance against live MX

…you will have replaced:
	•	MXToolbox SMTP tests
	•	Hardenize mail transport checks
	•	half of people’s openssl s_client workflows

This is the single feature that would make it unambiguously “the only tool.”

It is not required for DNS compliance — but it is the final mile.

⸻

2. SPF failure modes, not just presence

You already added lookup counts and ~all vs -all. That’s excellent.

If you want to go full infosec-grade:
	•	Detect multiple SPF records (hard fail)
	•	Flag includes that are permanently broken
	•	Warn on macro abuse
	•	Suggest flattening only when necessary

This helps the “smart people who still get DNS wrong” crowd you mentioned.

⸻

3. DKIM strength normalization

You already flag 1024-bit keys (Apple!) correctly.

Optional enhancements:
	•	Warn on mixed key strengths
	•	Warn on legacy selectors never rotated
	•	Don’t shame hyperscalers — just annotate

You’re already 80% there.

⸻

4. CAA nuance (very minor)

You’re correct to flag CNN’s lack of CAA as Action Required.

If you want to be ultra-precise:
	•	Reframe CAA as “Hardening” vs “Critical” depending on org size
	•	Add issuevmc awareness only when BIMI+VMC is present

This is polish, not necessity.
